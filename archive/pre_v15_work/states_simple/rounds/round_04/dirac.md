# DIRAC — Round 4 — States.tex

**Verdict: NOT-YET**

## Lens

Is the three-home structure inevitable — no unexplained special case? The bar: a
competent engineer who has never seen this problem reads the third home as forced,
not chosen.

## What is now beautiful

The frame is a clean 2×2: dependency ∈ {unit, (holder,unit)} × authorship ∈
{external authority, ledger's own}, three cells filled, one predicted-empty. Three
of the four boundary arguments are inevitable and the empty cell is genuinely
Dirac-shaped (an absence the formalism forces):

- **Position keyed by (holder, unit)** — buyer +1000 / seller −1000 collapse under a
  unit key. Forced.
- **Status keyed by unit** — per-holder copies of one number drift apart, a
  reconciliation break by construction. Forced.
- **Empty cell** — every position is sourced from the ledger's own move events; no
  external per-(holder, unit) authority exists to version. The managed-account
  apparent counterexample dissolving into a (client, mandate-unit) position
  (−1/+1, summing to zero) is the most beautiful move in the page — apparent
  opposites unified. Forced.

My round-3 residue — the split hanging on the access-pattern claim "only ever needed
as the current projection" — is gone. The revision took the fix I proposed: it
re-grounds the split on provenance and removes the usage sentence. Good.

## The residue (located, actionable)

**The second axis is named "authorship (external authority vs the ledger's own
record)," but the document's own Status examples are externally authored — so the
stated axis does not carve Terms from Status. The carve survives; its stated reason
does not.**

Locations:
- §The Answer, lines 53–67 (axis defined as authorship; Status examples listed).
- §Why Three ¶3, lines 110–121 ("Terms are separate from status because the two are
  authored differently").

The sharpest witness is internal to the page. Benchmark **identity** is Terms
(line 64); benchmark **level** is Status (line 67). Both are authored by the *same*
external index provider. An axis labelled "external authority vs ledger's own"
places two facts from one external author in opposite cells — so authorship is not
the thing separating them. The same leak repeats across the Status list: "last
settlement price" is the exchange's figure, "current weights" and "benchmark level"
are the index provider's, yet all three are declared "the ledger's own record"
(lines 66, 116). The number's author is external in every case.

What actually carves the two unit-keyed homes is a *retention semantics*, not a
source: Terms are **definitional and retroactively correctable** — a correction
restates what the terms always were, so the prior must be kept to audit what was
believed at the time (line 64, "the prior kept"); Status is **observational and
superseding** — each settlement is a new forward fact, so the current value stands
and any prior is recovered by replay. That criterion cleanly separates benchmark
identity (a correctable definition) from benchmark level (a superseding
observation), and it does not depend on who originated the number. The page's own
phrase "corrected only by appending a version" (line 64) is the real axis; it is
present but not load-bearing, while "authorship" — which is — is contradicted by the
examples.

The §Why-Three concession makes this exact: "what differs is the shape each home
keeps … and that shape follows authorship; a combined cell is spellable, so the
split is by provenance, not necessity" (lines 116–121). The cardinality of the
answer — three homes, not two — rests on "provenance," and provenance is the axis
the examples falsify. A competent reader who notices that settlement prices and
index levels arrive from outside will ask why they are not external-authority
versioned Terms, and the page's stated criterion gives no answer.

This does not collapse the structure — the three homes are right. It means the third
home is justified by a criterion (authorship-by-source) that the page's own
instances violate, which is precisely the unexplained special case the bar forbids.

## Fix (either suffices)

- **Rename the second axis to its retention semantics.** Make the carve
  *correctable definition (prior retained)* vs *superseding observation (current,
  prior by replay)*, and state explicitly that an externally-sourced number is still
  a ledger-authored Status record when the ledger records it as a superseding
  observation rather than mirroring the authority's correctable definition. Then
  benchmark identity vs benchmark level, and multiplier vs settlement price, fall
  out of the *stated* axis instead of contradicting it.
- **Or keep "authorship" but redefine it** as authorship-of-the-record (who is the
  system of record for *corrections*: the external authority for Terms, the ledger
  for Status) rather than authorship-of-the-number, and say so where the Status
  examples are introduced (line 66) so the exchange/index-sourced figures are not
  read as external-authority artifacts.

Until the axis that the page names is the axis that actually separates Terms from
Status, the third home reads as carved-correctly-for-an-unstated-reason, not
inevitable.
